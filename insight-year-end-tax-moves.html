The tax decisions to settle before December 31

Most of what determines your tax bill is locked in by year-end. A handful of moves are still on the table in December and gone by January — here's the short list worth reviewing.

April is when most people think about taxes. By then, almost nothing can be changed. The decisions that actually move your liability have a deadline of December 31 — and the weeks before it are the real planning season for business owners and high-income individuals.

This isn't an exhaustive list, and the right moves depend entirely on your situation. But these are the questions worth working through before the year closes.

Retirement contributions

Retirement plans are among the most powerful tax tools an owner has, and several have year-end deadlines for setup or funding. A Solo 401(k), SEP-IRA, or even a defined-benefit plan can move a meaningful amount of income into tax-deferred territory — but some have to be established before December 31 to count for the year. If you've had a strong year, this is usually the first place to look.

Income and expense timing

If you have flexibility over when income lands or expenses hit, year-end is when you use it:

  • Defer income into next year if you expect to be in a lower bracket, or accelerate it if the reverse is true.
  • Prepay deductible expenses — but only where it genuinely makes sense, not just to spend money.
  • Time large purchases so depreciation or expensing lands in the year it helps most.

The point is to look across this year and next, not just chase a deduction in isolation.

Equipment and capital purchases

If the business genuinely needs equipment, placing it in service before year-end can accelerate the deduction through Section 179 or bonus depreciation. The key word is need — buying something for the write-off alone is spending a dollar to save a fraction of it.

Entity and compensation moves

Some structural decisions need to be made before year-end to apply to the current year. If you're weighing an S-corp election, or your owner compensation needs adjusting, those are time-sensitive — and worth coordinating with your reasonable compensation plan rather than handling in isolation.

Estimated taxes and withholding

Year-end is the moment to true up estimates and withholding so you're not hit with an underpayment penalty — or floating the government an interest-free loan. A quick projection tells you where you'll land and whether a final estimate is needed.

None of this works as a last-minute scramble. It works as a planned review with a few weeks of runway — which is exactly why we build it into the year.

This kind of year-end review is part of the planning cycle we run with clients, grounded in books we keep current all year so the numbers are real when it's time to act. If you've never had a genuine year-end planning conversation, you're likely leaving options on the table.
