When does an S-corp election actually make sense?

It's the most over-recommended move in small-business tax. Sometimes it saves real money. Sometimes it costs you more than it saves. The difference is the math — and almost no one runs it before electing.

If you own a profitable business taxed as a sole proprietorship or LLC, someone has probably told you to "become an S-corp to save on taxes." It's common advice because it's sometimes right. But it's given far too freely — usually without the one thing that determines whether it helps you specifically: your numbers.

Here's how to think about the decision the way a tax advisor actually does.

What the S-corp election really changes

An S-corp is not a type of business — it's a tax election an LLC or corporation makes with the IRS. The mechanics that matter for tax come down to one thing: how your profit is taxed for Social Security and Medicare (self-employment tax).

As a sole proprietor or standard LLC, essentially all of your net profit is subject to self-employment tax (15.3% up to the Social Security wage base, then 2.9%+ above it). As an S-corp, you split your income into two buckets:

  • A reasonable salary you pay yourself as a W-2 employee — subject to payroll taxes.
  • The remaining profit, taken as a distribution — not subject to self-employment tax.

That second bucket is where the savings live. The catch: the salary has to be reasonable, and the election brings real costs and obligations that eat into the benefit.

The rough math

Consider a hypothetical owner with $150,000 of net profit (figures here are illustrative, not a promise). As a sole proprietor, most of that profit is exposed to self-employment tax. As an S-corp paying, say, a $90,000 reasonable salary, only the salary carries payroll tax — the remaining ~$60,000 distribution avoids the 15.3%/2.9% layer.

The savings are real, but they're a slice of the distribution — not your whole income. The bigger and more durable your profit above a reasonable salary, the more the election is worth.

Now subtract the costs the election creates, because they're not trivial.

The costs people forget to subtract

  • Payroll setup and processing — you now have to run formal payroll, file payroll returns, and issue yourself a W-2.
  • A separate business return — an 1120-S is more involved (and more expensive to prepare) than a Schedule C.
  • State-level costs — some states impose franchise taxes, fees, or their own treatment that erodes the federal benefit.
  • Tighter compliance — reasonable compensation, basis tracking, and distribution rules all have to be handled correctly.

For a business with modest profit, those fixed costs can equal or exceed the tax saved. That's the case the "just become an S-corp" crowd never checks.

The rule that decides it: reasonable compensation

The entire strategy rests on paying yourself a reasonable salary for the work you do. Pay yourself too little to inflate the tax-free distribution, and you've created audit exposure — reasonable compensation is one of the first things the IRS examines on S-corp returns.

Done right, the salary is defensible: benchmarked to what your role would earn, documented, and consistent. This is exactly the kind of judgment call that's worth getting from someone who will stand behind it — which is the heart of our tax planning and advisory work.

So — does it make sense for you?

An S-corp election is more likely to pay off when:

  • Your profit is comfortably above a reasonable salary for your role, with room to spare for distributions.
  • That profit is durable — not a one-off spike that disappears next year.
  • You're prepared to run payroll and keep clean books (or have someone do it for you).

It's more likely to be a wash — or a net cost — when profit is modest, volatile, or barely above what you'd have to pay yourself anyway.

The honest answer is that it depends on numbers only you have. The right move is to model it both ways before you elect, including the added costs, and revisit it as your profit grows.
