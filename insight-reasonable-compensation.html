Reasonable compensation: the line where S-corp planning is won or lost

The S-corp tax benefit depends entirely on one number — the salary you pay yourself. Set it well and the savings hold. Set it carelessly and you've handed the IRS its first question.

If you run an S-corp, you already know the headline: paying yourself a salary plus distributions can cut your self-employment tax, because distributions aren't subject to it. What gets glossed over is the rule that makes the whole thing work — your salary has to be reasonable compensation for the work you actually do.

Get this number right and the strategy is solid. Get greedy and pay yourself a token salary to maximize tax-free distributions, and you've created exactly the exposure the IRS looks for first.

What "reasonable" actually means

The IRS doesn't publish a formula, which is what makes this hard. The standard is what you would have to pay someone else to do your job — your role, your hours, your responsibilities, in your industry and market. An owner who runs the whole company can't reasonably claim a part-time bookkeeper's salary.

Factors that matter include:

  • Your training, experience, and duties
  • The time and effort you devote to the business
  • What comparable businesses pay for similar roles
  • How much of the company's income is from your personal services versus capital or other employees

Why too-low a salary backfires

Underpaying yourself is one of the most common S-corp mistakes — and one of the easiest for the IRS to challenge. If they reclassify distributions as wages, you owe the back payroll taxes plus penalties and interest. The "savings" evaporate and then some.

The goal isn't the lowest possible salary. It's the lowest defensible salary — one you can support with evidence if anyone asks.

How to set it defensibly

Good reasonable-compensation work looks like this:

  • Benchmark it. Use real compensation data for your role and region, not a round number or a flat percentage of profit.
  • Document the reasoning. Keep a record of how you arrived at the figure — the data, the duties, the rationale.
  • Revisit it annually. As the business and your role change, the number should change with them.
  • Keep distributions consistent with the salary you've set, and run actual payroll — don't reconstruct it at year-end.

This is judgment work, and it's exactly the kind of call worth getting from a CPA who will stand behind it. It's central to our tax planning and advisory engagements, and it sits alongside the broader question of whether an S-corp election makes sense for you at all.

The bottom line

Reasonable compensation isn't a box to check — it's the foundation the entire S-corp strategy rests on. Done carefully, it's quietly one of the most valuable pieces of planning a business owner can get. Done carelessly, it turns a smart structure into an audit risk.

If you're not confident your salary would hold up — or you've never actually benchmarked it — that's worth a conversation before the next filing, not after.
