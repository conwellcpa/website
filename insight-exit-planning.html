The tax work to do years before you sell

By the time there's a buyer at the table, most of the tax outcome is already locked in. The planning that moves the number happens two to five years earlier — while there's still time to shape it.

Selling a business is often the single largest financial event of an owner's life. It's also the one where the gap between good and poor tax planning is widest — and where that gap is decided long before the deal closes.

Most owners start thinking about the tax side once an offer is real. By then, the structural choices that matter most are already made. The valuable work lives in the two-to-five-year window before a sale.

Why timing decides so much

The tax treatment of a sale depends on things that can't be changed quickly: your entity type, how long you've held the business, how the deal is structured, and where you live. Several of the best strategies require a holding period or advance setup — start them the year of the sale and you've missed them entirely.

Exit planning is one of the clearest cases where a decision made early is worth far more than the same decision made late — and often can't be made late at all.

What's on the table early

  • Entity structure. How the business is organized shapes whether a sale is taxed as capital gain or ordinary income, and how a buyer can structure the purchase. Restructuring takes time and can't be rushed at the finish line.
  • Asset vs. stock sale. Buyers and sellers usually prefer opposite structures for tax reasons. Understanding the trade-offs early strengthens your position in negotiation.
  • Qualified small business stock (QSBS). For eligible C-corp stock, a multi-year holding period can exclude a significant share of gain — but only if the setup and timeline are right, well in advance.
  • State residency. Where you live when you sell can materially change the state tax bill. Any change has to be genuine and established over time, not staged the month before closing.
  • Spreading the gain. Installment sales, timing, and charitable strategies can smooth a one-year spike — each needs to be planned, not improvised.

The cost of waiting

When planning starts only once a deal is live, the options collapse to whatever's left. Holding periods can't be met retroactively. Restructuring mid-deal can create its own tax cost or spook a buyer. Residency can't be backdated. The result is a larger tax bill that was, in principle, avoidable — the owner just ran out of runway.

How we approach it

For owners who are even loosely thinking about a transition, we build the tax groundwork into the ongoing planning relationship — reviewing structure, watching the clock on anything with a holding period, and modeling what different exit paths would actually net you. It connects directly to the same entity and structure decisions that shape the business day to day.

You don't need a deal on the table to start. In fact, that's the whole point: the best time to plan your exit is well before you need to.
